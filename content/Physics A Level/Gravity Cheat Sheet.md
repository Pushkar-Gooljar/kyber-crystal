## Gravitational Field

>[!tip] Concept: Field of Force
>- A field of force is a region in space where a particle experiences a force.
>- Fields of force are useful to describe non-contact forces, such as gravity and electromagnetism.

>[!info] Definition: Gravitational Field
>- A gravitational field is a region of space where a mass experiences a force.
>- **Gravitational field strength (g)** at a point is the gravitational force exerted per unit mass on a small test mass placed at that point.

### Representing Gravitational Fields

>[!success] Field Lines
>- **Direction:** Field lines show the direction of the gravitational force on a test mass.
>- **Strength:** The spacing of the field lines indicates the strength of the field. Closer lines mean a stronger field.
>
>#### Field Pattern for a Planet (e.g., Earth)
>- **Radial:** The field lines are directed radially inwards.
>- **Direction:** They point towards the centre of the planet.
>- **Surface Approximation:** For small changes in height near the surface, the field lines are approximately parallel, equally spaced, and perpendicular to the surface.

<br>

### Gravitational Field Strength (g)

>[!math] Formula: Gravitational Field Strength
> Derived from Newton's Law of Gravitation and the definition of a field.
> $$\begin{align} \\
\text{g} &= \frac{\text{Force}}{mass} = \frac{F}{m} \\ \\
\mathrm{g} &= \left(\frac{\mathrm{GMm}}{\mathrm{r^2}}\right) \times \frac{1}{\mathrm{m}} \\ \\
\mathrm{g} &= \frac{\mathrm{GM}}{\mathrm{r^2}}
\end{align}$$

>[!tip] Why 'g' is constant near the Earth's surface
>- For small changes in height (`h`), the distance from the centre of the Earth (`r = R + h`) is approximately equal to the Earth's radius (`R`).
>- The change in height is negligible compared to the radius of the planet.
>- Therefore, `g` can be considered constant.

<br>

## Gravitational Force

>[!info] Newton's Law of Gravitation
>- The gravitational force between two point masses is directly proportional to the product of their masses and inversely proportional to the square of the distance between their centres.
>- The force is always attractive.
>
>For a uniform sphere, its mass can be considered to be a point mass at its centre.

>[!math] Formula: Newton's Law of Gravitation
> $$F = \frac{Gm_1m_2}{r^2}$$
>Where `G` is the universal gravitational constant ($6.67 \times 10^{-11} \, \text{N m}^2 \text{kg}^{-2}$).

<br>

## Circular Orbits in Gravitational Fields

>[!info] Analysis of Circular Orbits
>- The gravitational force of attraction provides the necessary **centripetal force** that keeps an object (e.g., a satellite) in a stable circular orbit.

>[!math] Equating Forces
> By equating the gravitational force with the centripetal force formulae, we can analyse the orbit.
> $$\begin{align} \\ \text{Gravitational Force} &= \text{Centripetal Force} \\ \\ \frac{GMm}{r^2} &= \frac{mv^2}{r} = mr\omega^2 \end{align}$$

>[!success] Geostationary Orbits
> A satellite in a geostationary orbit has specific conditions:
>- It has an orbital period of **24 hours**.
>- It orbits directly **above the Equator**.
>- It orbits from **west to east** (in the same direction as the Earth's rotation).
>- As a result, it remains at the **same fixed point** above the Earth’s surface.

<br>

## Gravitational Potential & Energy

>[!info] Definition: Gravitational Potential ($\phi$)
>- The gravitational potential at a point is the **work done per unit mass** in bringing a small test mass **from infinity to that point**.

>[!math] Formula: Gravitational Potential
> $$\phi = -\frac{GM}{r}$$

<br>

>[!info] Definition: Gravitational Potential Energy ($E_p$)
> - The gravitational potential energy of two masses is the **work done** in bringing the masses **from infinity to their current separation**.
> - It is related to potential by: $E_p = m\phi$

>[!math] Formula: Gravitational Potential Energy
>$$E_p = -\frac{GMm}{r}$$

>[!warning] Why are Gravitational Potential and GPE always negative?
>- **Zero Point:** Gravitational potential and GPE are defined as being zero at an infinite distance away.
>- **Attractive Force:** The gravitational force is always attractive.
>- **Work Done:** To move a mass from a point in the field *away* towards infinity, work must be done *on* the mass (energy is put into the system). Therefore, any mass within the field has less energy than it does at infinity, so its potential and GPE must be negative.
