## 12.1 Nitrogen and Sulfur

### 12.1.1: Nitrogen Gas ($N_2$) Reactivity

-   **Description:** Nitrogen gas is very unreactive / inert.
-   **Explanation (Two Key Reasons):**
    1.  **Strong Triple Bond:** The $N \equiv N$ triple covalent bond has a very high bond energy (945 kJ/mol). A large amount of energy (high activation energy) is required to break this bond before nitrogen can react.
    2.  **Lack of Polarity:** The $N_2$ molecule is non-polar. This means it lacks a permanent dipole, making it less susceptible to attack by nucleophiles or electrophiles.

> [!check] Comparison with CO
> Even though CO also has a strong triple bond, it is much more reactive than $N_2$. This is because CO is a **polar molecule** (due to the difference in electronegativity between C and O), making it susceptible to attack.

### 12.1.2: Ammonia ($NH_3$) and Ammonium ($NH_4^+$)

#### (a) Basicity of Ammonia
-   **Description:** Ammonia is a **weak base**.
-   **Brønsted-Lowry Theory:** A base is a **proton acceptor**. Ammonia has a lone pair of electrons on the nitrogen atom, which can accept a proton ($H^+$).
-   **Equation:** $$NH_{3(aq)} + H_2O_{(l)} \rightleftharpoons NH_{4(aq)}^{+} + OH^{-}_{(aq)}$$

#### (b) The Ammonium Ion
-   **Formation:** It is formed when ammonia accepts a proton from an acid. The lone pair on the nitrogen forms a **dative covalent bond** with the $H^+$ ion.
-   **Equation:** $$NH_{3} + H^{+} \rightarrow NH_{4}^{+}$$
-   **Structure:** Tetrahedral shape with bond angles of 109.5°. All four N-H bonds are identical in length and strength once formed.

#### (c) Displacement of Ammonia from Ammonium Salts
-   **Principle:** A stronger base will displace a weaker base from its salt.
-   **Reaction:** To produce ammonia gas from an ammonium salt (e.g., $NH_4Cl$), you must heat it with a strong base (e.g., $NaOH$, $Ca(OH)_2$, $CaO$).
-   **Equation:** $$NH_4Cl_{(s)} + NaOH_{(aq)} \rightarrow NaCl_{(aq)} + H_2O_{(l)} + NH_{3(g)}$$
-   **Ionic Equation:** $$NH_{4(aq)}^{+} + OH^{-}_{(aq)} \rightarrow H_2O_{(l)} + NH_{3(g)}$$
-   **Test for Ammonium Ions:** Warm the sample with NaOH(aq). The gas produced will be ammonia, which can be identified because it is alkaline (turns damp red litmus paper blue).

### 12.1.3 - 12.1.5: Nitrogen Oxides ($NO_x$) and their Environmental Impact

#### Occurrences of Nitrogen Oxides ($NO$ and $NO_2$)
-   **Natural Source:** **Lightning strikes**. The high energy of lightning provides the activation energy to break the strong $N \equiv N$ bond, allowing nitrogen to react with atmospheric oxygen. $$N_{2(g)} + O_{2(g)} \xrightarrow{\text{lightning}} 2NO_{(g)}$$
-   **Man-Made Source:** **Internal combustion engines** of cars. The high temperature and pressure inside the engine provide the energy for the same reaction.

#### Catalytic Removal from Exhaust Gases
-   **Problem:** $NO_x$ are pollutants.
-   **Solution:** **Catalytic converters** in car exhausts.
-   **Catalyst:** Platinum (Pt), Palladium (Pd), or Rhodium (Rh) finely coated on a ceramic honeycomb structure.
-   **Reaction:** The converter uses the toxic gases $CO$ and $NO$ to convert each other into harmless products.
    $$2CO_{(g)} + 2NO_{(g)} \rightarrow 2CO_{2(g)} + N_{2(g)}$$

#### Environmental Consequences of $NO_x$

> [!warning] Key Environmental Roles of $NO_x$
> Nitrogen oxides are involved in two major environmental problems: **Photochemical Smog** and **Acid Rain**.

1.  **Photochemical Smog** (Syllabus Point 4)
    -   **Process:** In the presence of sunlight, $NO_2$ reacts with **unburned hydrocarbons** (from incomplete combustion in car engines).
    -   **Product:** This reaction forms **Peroxyacetyl Nitrate (PAN)**, a major component of photochemical smog.
    -   **Consequences:** Smog causes respiratory problems and damages plants.

2.  **Acid Rain** (Syllabus Point 5)
    $NO_x$ contribute to acid rain in two ways:

    **(a) Direct Formation of Nitric Acid**
    -   First, $NO$ is oxidised in the air to $NO_2$: $$2NO_{(g)} + O_{2(g)} \rightarrow 2NO_{2(g)}$$
    -   Then, nitrogen dioxide reacts with water and oxygen in the atmosphere to form nitric acid, a component of acid rain.
        $$4NO_{2(g)} + 2H_2O_{(l)} + O_{2(g)} \rightarrow 4HNO_{3(aq)}$$

    **(b) Catalytic Role in the Formation of Sulfuric Acid**
    -   Sulfur dioxide ($SO_2$) is the primary pollutant responsible for acid rain, produced from burning fossil fuels containing sulfur. Its oxidation to $SO_3$ is slow.
    -   **Role of $NO_2$:** Nitrogen dioxide acts as a **homogeneous catalyst** to speed up the oxidation of $SO_2$ to $SO_3$.
    -   **Step 1 (Catalyst used):** $NO_2$ oxidises $SO_2$.
        $$SO_{2(g)} + NO_{2(g)} \rightarrow SO_{3(g)} + NO_{(g)}$$
    -   **Step 2 (Catalyst regenerated):** The $NO$ produced is rapidly re-oxidised by air back to $NO_2$.
        $$2NO_{(g)} + O_{2(g)} \rightarrow 2NO_{2(g)}$$
    -   **Final step:** The $SO_3$ then dissolves in water droplets to form sulfuric acid.
        $$SO_{3(g)} + H_2O_{(l)} \rightarrow H_2SO_{4(aq)}$$
    -   **Definition:** $NO_2$ is a **homogeneous catalyst** because it is in the same phase (gas) as the reactants ($SO_2$ and $O_2$).
