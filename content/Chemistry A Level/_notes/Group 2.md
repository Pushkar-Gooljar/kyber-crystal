## 10 & 27: Group 2

### 10.1.1 & 10.1.2: Reactions of the Elements & Compounds

#### Reactions of Group 2 Metals (M)

| Reagent | Conditions | Observations | General Equation |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Oxygen** | Strong heating | Bright white flame/light, white solid/smoke forms. | $$2M_{(s)} + O_{2(g)} \rightarrow 2MO_{(s)}$$ |
| **Water** | Varies down group | Fizzing (effervescence), metal dissolves/disappears, colourless solution forms. Reactivity increases down the group (Ba > Sr > Ca > Mg). | $$M_{(s)} + 2H_2O_{(l)} \rightarrow M(OH)_{2(aq)} + H_{2(g)}$$ |
| **Steam** | For Mg (Vigorous) | Mg glows brightly, white solid forms. | $$Mg_{(s)} + H_2O_{(g)} \rightarrow MgO_{(s)} + H_{2(g)}$$ |
| **Dilute HCl** | Room Temp | Vigorous fizzing, metal dissolves. | $$M_{(s)} + 2HCl_{(aq)} \rightarrow MCl_{2(aq)} + H_{2(g)}$$ |
| **Dilute H₂SO₄** | Room Temp | Initial fizzing. Reaction stops for Ca, Sr, Ba. | $$M_{(s)} + H_2SO_4{(aq)} \rightarrow MSO_{4(s/aq)} + H_{2(g)}$$ |

> [!warning] Special Case: Reaction with H₂SO₄
> The reaction of Ca, Sr, and Ba with sulfuric acid starts but quickly stops. This is because an **insoluble layer of the sulfate** ($CaSO_4$, $SrSO_4$, $BaSO_4$) coats the surface of the metal, preventing further reaction. This is a very common exam question (e.g., Q#219).

#### Reactions of Group 2 Compounds

These are simple acid-base neutralisation reactions.
-   **Oxides:** Basic. `$MO + 2HCl \rightarrow MCl_2 + H_2O$`
-   **Hydroxides:** Basic. `$M(OH)_2 + 2HCl \rightarrow MCl_2 + 2H_2O$`
-   **Carbonates:** Basic. `$MCO_3 + 2HCl \rightarrow MCl_2 + H_2O + CO_2$`
    -   **Key Observation:** Effervescence / fizzing / bubbles due to $CO_2$ gas production.

### 10.1.3 & 27.1.1: Thermal Stability of Carbonates and Nitrates

This is a core topic. The key trend and its detailed explanation are essential.

#### The Trend
-   **Trend:** The **thermal stability** of both carbonates and nitrates **increases** down Group 2.
-   **Meaning:** A higher temperature is required to decompose the compounds as you go down the group. (e.g., Barium carbonate is more stable than magnesium carbonate).

#### Decomposition Equations

-   **Carbonates:** Decompose to the metal oxide and carbon dioxide.
    $$MCO_{3(s)} \rightarrow MO_{(s)} + CO_{2(g)}$$
-   **Nitrates:** Decompose to the metal oxide, nitrogen dioxide, and oxygen.
    $$2M(NO_3)_{2(s)} \rightarrow 2MO_{(s)} + 4NO_{2(g)} + O_{2(g)}$$
    -   **Key Observation:** Brown gas ($NO_2$) is evolved.

#### A2 Explanation: The Effect of Cation Polarisation

> [!info] The Logic for Increased Stability Down the Group
> 1.  **Cationic Radius:** As you descend Group 2, the ionic radius of the $M^{2+}$ cation **increases**.
> 2.  **Charge Density:** Since the charge is constant (+2), the charge density **decreases** down the group.
> 3.  **Polarising Power:** The ability of the cation to distort the electron cloud of the anion (its polarising power) **decreases** as the charge density decreases.
> 4.  **Anion Distortion:** The large carbonate ($CO_3^{2-}$) or nitrate ($NO_3^−$) anions are **less polarised / distorted** by the larger cations (like $Ba^{2+}$) than by the smaller cations (like $Mg^{2+}$).
> 5.  **Bond Weakening:** This polarisation weakens the covalent bonds within the anion (the C-O or N-O bonds).
> 6.  **Conclusion:** Since the bonds in the anion are weakened *less* down the group, **more energy (a higher temperature) is required** to break them and cause decomposition. Therefore, thermal stability increases.

### 10.1.5 & 27.1.2: Solubility of Hydroxides and Sulfates

There are two opposite trends that you must know.

#### The Trends
-   **Group 2 Hydroxides, M(OH)₂:** Solubility **increases** down the group.
    -   $Mg(OH)_2$ is 'sparingly soluble' / almost insoluble.
    -   $Ba(OH)_2$ is the most soluble.
-   **Group 2 Sulfates, MSO₄:** Solubility **decreases** down the group.
    -   $MgSO_4$ is soluble.
    -   $BaSO_4$ is insoluble (forms a dense white precipitate).

> [!check] Using the Solubility Trends
> These trends are the basis for chemical tests. To distinguish between two Group 2 ions (e.g., $Mg^{2+}$ and $Ba^{2+}$), you can add a reagent that forms a precipitate with one but not the other.
> - **Test:** Add dilute $H_2SO_4$ (or any soluble sulfate like $Na_2SO_4$).
> - **Result:** A white precipitate ($BaSO_4$) will form with the $Ba^{2+}$ solution, but no change will be seen with the $Mg^{2+}$ solution.

#### A2 Explanation: The Balance of Enthalpy Changes

Solubility is governed by the **enthalpy of solution ($\Delta H_{sol}$)**, which is a balance between the energy required to break the lattice (**Lattice Enthalpy, $\Delta H_{latt}$**) and the energy released when ions are hydrated (**Hydration Enthalpy, $\Delta H_{hyd}$**).

-   **General Principle:** As you descend Group 2, the $M^{2+}$ cation gets larger. This causes both the $|\Delta H_{latt}|$ and $|\Delta H_{hyd}|$ to **decrease** (become less exothermic), because the electrostatic attractions are weaker over a greater distance.
-   **The Deciding Factor:** The *relative change* in these two values determines the solubility trend.

| Anion Size | Anion | Which enthalpy changes more? | Effect on $\Delta H_{sol}$ | Solubility Trend |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Small** | **$OH^{-}$** | **Lattice Enthalpy** ($\Delta H_{latt}$) decreases more rapidly than $\Delta H_{hyd}$. | The energy cost to break the lattice drops more significantly than the energy payoff from hydration. Thus, $\Delta H_{sol}$ becomes **more exothermic** (more negative) down the group. | **Increases** |
| **Large** | **$SO_4^{2-}$** | **Hydration Enthalpy** ($\Delta H_{hyd}$) decreases more rapidly than $\Delta H_{latt}$. | The energy payoff from hydration drops more significantly than the energy cost to break the lattice. Thus, $\Delta H_{sol}$ becomes **more endothermic** (more positive) down the group. | **Decreases** |
