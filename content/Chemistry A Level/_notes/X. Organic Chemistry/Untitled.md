## 23.3 Entropy Change, ΔS

### 1. Defining Entropy (S)

#### What is Entropy?
- **Formal Definition:** Entropy, symbol **S**, is a measure of the **number of possible arrangements** of particles and their energy in a given system. It quantifies how spread out or distributed the energy is.
- **Simpler Analogy:** Entropy is often described as a measure of the **disorder or randomness** of a system.

#### Key Points about Entropy
- **Higher Entropy = More Disorder:** A system with more disorder has more possible arrangements for its particles and energy, and therefore a higher entropy value.
- **Tendency towards Maximum Entropy:** Systems naturally tend to move towards a state of higher entropy (more disorder).
- **Standard Entropy (S⦵):**
    - This is the entropy of **one mole** of a substance under standard conditions.
    - **Standard Conditions:** 298 K (25 °C) and 100 kPa.
    - **Units:** Joules per Kelvin per mole (**J K⁻¹ mol⁻¹**).
    - Note: Unlike standard enthalpy of formation, the standard entropy of an element in its standard state is **not** zero. All substances have positive entropy values at temperatures above absolute zero (0 K).

### 2. Predicting and Explaining the Sign of Entropy Change (ΔS)

The sign of ΔS tells us whether the system has become more ordered or more disordered.
- **ΔS is positive (+ve):** Entropy has increased (more disorder).
- **ΔS is negative (-ve):** Entropy has decreased (more order).

#### (a) During a Change in State (and Dissolving)

The state of matter is a primary factor affecting entropy.

[Diagram showing ordered particles in a solid, more disordered particles in a liquid, and highly disordered, far-apart particles in a gas]

- **General Trend:** Entropy increases as you go from solid to liquid to gas.
    **S(solid) < S(liquid) << S(gas)**

- **Melting (Solid → Liquid):**
    - Particles break free from the fixed, ordered lattice and can move past each other.
    - Disorder increases, so **ΔS is positive**.

- **Boiling (Liquid → Gas):**
    - Particles break their intermolecular forces and move randomly and far apart.
    - There is a very large increase in disorder, so **ΔS is large and positive**.

- **Dissolving (Solid → Aqueous):**
    - The ordered crystal lattice of the solid breaks down.
    - The ions or molecules become mobile and spread out in the solvent.
    - Disorder increases, so **ΔS is positive**.

- **Reverse Processes:**
    - **Freezing (Liquid → Solid):** Particles become fixed in an ordered lattice. Disorder decreases, **ΔS is negative**.
    - **Condensing (Gas → Liquid):** Particles come closer together with less freedom of movement. Disorder decreases, **ΔS is large and negative**.
    - **Crystallisation (Aqueous → Solid):** Mobile ions form an ordered lattice. Disorder decreases, **ΔS is negative**.

#### (b) During a Temperature Change

- **Increasing Temperature:**
    - Particles gain kinetic energy, causing them to move faster (translating, rotating, and vibrating more vigorously).
    - This increases the number of ways the energy can be distributed among the particles.
    - Entropy increases, so **ΔS is positive**.

- **Decreasing Temperature:**
    - Particles lose kinetic energy and move less.
    - Disorder decreases.
    - Entropy decreases, so **ΔS is negative**.

[Graph of Entropy vs. Temperature, showing a gradual increase within each state (solid, liquid, gas) and sharp jumps at the melting point and boiling point]

#### (c) During a Reaction (Change in Gaseous Molecules)

For chemical reactions, the **change in the number of moles of gas** is the most important factor in predicting the sign of ΔS. Gases have vastly higher entropy than solids or liquids.

- **Rule 1: Moles of Gas Increase**
    - If a reaction produces more moles of gas than it consumes, the system becomes significantly more disordered.
    - **ΔS is positive**.
    - *Example:* CaCO₃(s) → CaO(s) + CO₂(g)
      - (0 moles of gas → 1 mole of gas)

- **Rule 2: Moles of Gas Decrease**
    - If a reaction consumes more moles of gas than it produces, the system becomes significantly more ordered.
    - **ΔS is negative**.
    - *Example:* N₂(g) + 3H₂(g) → 2NH₃(g)
      - (4 moles of gas → 2 moles of gas)

- **Rule 3: Moles of Gas Stay Constant**
    - The entropy change will likely be small. The sign is harder to predict and depends on the relative complexity of the molecules involved.

### 3. Calculating the Standard Entropy Change of a Reaction (ΔS⦵)

The standard entropy change of a reaction (ΔS⦵) can be calculated using the standard entropies (S⦵) of the reactants and products.

#### The Formula
The formula is:
**ΔS⦵<sub>reaction</sub> = ΣS⦵(products) – ΣS⦵(reactants)**

Where:
- **ΣS⦵(products)** is the sum of the standard entropies of all the products.
- **ΣS⦵(reactants)** is the sum of the standard entropies of all the reactants.

**Important:** You must multiply the S⦵ value for each substance by its stoichiometric coefficient (the balancing number) in the balanced chemical equation.

#### Step-by-Step Method
1.  Write the full, balanced chemical equation.
2.  Calculate ΣS⦵(products) by adding the S⦵ values for each product, multiplied by their balancing numbers.
3.  Calculate ΣS⦵(reactants) by adding the S⦵ values for each reactant, multiplied by their balancing numbers.
4.  Subtract the total for the reactants from the total for the products.

#### Worked Example

**Question:** Calculate the standard entropy change of the system for the formation of magnesium oxide.
**Reaction:** 2Mg(s) + O₂(g) → 2MgO(s)

**Given Standard Entropies (S⦵):**
- S⦵ [Mg(s)] = 32.6 J K⁻¹ mol⁻¹
- S⦵ [O₂(g)] = 205.0 J K⁻¹ mol⁻¹
- S⦵ [MgO(s)] = 27.0 J K⁻¹ mol⁻¹ *(Note: Using a common literature value here which might differ slightly from the image)*

**Step 1: Write the formula**
ΔS⦵ = ΣS⦵(products) – ΣS⦵(reactants)

**Step 2: Calculate ΣS⦵(products)**
ΣS⦵(products) = 2 × S⦵[MgO(s)]
ΣS⦵(products) = 2 × 27.0 = **54.0 J K⁻¹ mol⁻¹**

**Step 3: Calculate ΣS⦵(reactants)**
ΣS⦵(reactants) = (2 × S⦵[Mg(s)]) + (1 × S⦵[O₂(g)])
ΣS⦵(reactants) = (2 × 32.6) + (205.0)
ΣS⦵(reactants) = 65.2 + 205.0 = **270.2 J K⁻¹ mol⁻¹**

**Step 4: Calculate ΔS⦵**
ΔS⦵ = 54.0 - 270.2
**ΔS⦵ = -216.2 J K⁻¹ mol⁻¹**

**Interpretation:** The final value is negative. This makes sense because the reaction consumes 1 mole of highly disordered gas (O₂) and produces only solids, resulting in a large decrease in the overall disorder of the system.
