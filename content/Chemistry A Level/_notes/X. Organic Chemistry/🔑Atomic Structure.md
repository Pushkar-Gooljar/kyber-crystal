## Isotopes
- Isotopes are atoms of the **same element**
- Having the **same number of protons** but **different number of neutrons**
### Similarities between isotopes
- Isotopes of the same element have the **same chemical properties**.
- This is because isotopes of the same element have the **same number of electrons** and **same electronic configuration**, and chemical properties depend on the arrangement of electrons.
- This means that isotopes of the same element participate in the same reactions.

### Differences between isotopes
- Isotopes of the same element have **different physical properties**. (e.g. density, mass)
- This is because isotopes of the same element have **different number of neutrons**

## Electronic Configuration & Orbitals

### Principal Quantum Shell
- The Principal Quantum Shell designates an electron's primary energy level.
- The principal quantum number, $n$ indicates the shell's size and energy.
- A principal quantum shell can contain $2n^2$ electrons.

### Subshells
- The principal quantum shell consists of a set of subshells each of which is of a different energy level.
- There are 4 types of subshells: s, p, d, f

### Shape of Orbitals
#### s orbitals
- The s orbitals are **spherical** in shape. 
- The **size of the s orbitals increases** with increasing principal quantum number.
	- For example, the s orbital of the third quantum shell (n=3) is bigger than the s orbital of the first quantum shell (n=1)

#### p orbitals
- The p orbitals are **dumbbell-shaped.**
- Every shell has three p orbitals except for the first one (n=1)
- The p orbitals occupy the x, y, and z axes and point at right angles to each other.
- The lobes of the p orbitals get **larger** and **longer** with increasing principal quantum number.

### Energy Level Diagram
- The **principal quantum shells** increase in energy with increasing principal quantum number. (e.g. n=3 is higher in energy than n=1)
- The subshells increase in energy as follows: **s < p < d < f**
>[!caution] Exception
>- The only exception to these rules is the 3d orbital which has slightly higher energy than the 4s orbital
>- Because of this, the 4s orbital is filled before the 3d orbital

- All the orbitals in the **same** sub-shell have the same energy and are said to be **degenerate**.

## Electronic Configurations

### The Rules for Filling Orbitals

**1. Aufbau Principle (Building Up)**
*   Electrons fill the lowest energy sub-shells first.
*   **Filling Order:** `1s → 2s → 2p → 3s → 3p → 4s → 3d → 4p → ...`
    *   **Note:** The **4s** sub-shell fills **before** the **3d** sub-shell because it is at a slightly lower energy level.

**2. Hund's Rule**
*   Electrons occupy orbitals in a sub-shell **singly** before any orbital is doubly occupied.
*   All electrons in singly occupied orbitals have parallel spins.
*   **Example:** Nitrogen (7 electrons) - `1s² 2s² 2p³`
    *   The three 2p electrons go into separate p orbitals: `[↑ ] [↑ ] [↑ ]` not `[↑↓] [↑ ] [ ]`

**3. Pauli Exclusion Principle**
*   An orbital can hold a maximum of **two electrons**.
*   If an orbital holds two electrons, they must have **opposite spins** (represented as ↑ and ↓).

---

### 3. How to Write a Configuration

**1. Full Electronic Configuration**
*   Find the atomic number (Z) = number of electrons in a neutral atom.
*   Fill the sub-shells according to the rules above until you have placed all the electrons.
*   **Example: Chlorine (Cl, Z=17)**
    *   `1s² 2s² 2p⁶ 3s² 3p⁵`  (Check electrons: 2+2+6+2+5 = 17)

**2. Shorthand (Noble Gas) Configuration**
*   Find the preceding noble gas in the periodic table.
*   Write its symbol in square brackets.
*   Continue writing the configuration for the remaining (valence) electrons.
*   **Example: Calcium (Ca, Z=20)**
    *   Previous noble gas: Argon (Ar, Z=18).
    *   Remaining electrons: 2.
    *   Configuration: `[Ar] 4s²`

---

### 4. Important Exceptions: Cr and Cu

The rules are broken to achieve a more stable arrangement. A **half-filled (d⁵)** or **fully-filled (d¹⁰)** d sub-shell is extra stable.

*   **Chromium (Cr, Z=24)**
    *   Expected: `[Ar] 4s² 3d⁴`
    *   **Actual:** `[Ar] 4s¹ 3d⁵`
    *   **Reason:** An electron is "promoted" from 4s to 3d to get a more stable, **half-filled** 3d sub-shell.

*   **Copper (Cu, Z=29)**
    *   Expected: `[Ar] 4s² 3d⁹`
    *   **Actual:** `[Ar] 4s¹ 3d¹⁰`
    *   **Reason:** An electron is "promoted" from 4s to 3d to get a more stable, **fully-filled** 3d sub-shell.

---

### 5. Configurations for Ions

**1. Anions (Negative Ions) - Gaining Electrons**
*   Add the extra electrons to the next available orbital, following the standard Aufbau principle.
*   **Example: Sulfide Ion (S²⁻)**
    *   S atom (Z=16): `[Ne] 3s² 3p⁴`
    *   Add 2 electrons: `[Ne] 3s² 3p⁶`

**2. Cations (Positive Ions) - Losing Electrons**

*   **For s- and p-block elements:** Remove electrons from the highest principal quantum number (*n*) shell first.
    *   **Example: Aluminium Ion (Al³⁺)**
        *   Al atom (Z=13): `[Ne] 3s² 3p¹`
        *   Remove 3 electrons from n=3: `[Ne]`

*   **For d-block (Transition Metal) elements: THE 4s FIRST RULE!**
    *   When forming positive ions, electrons are **REMOVED** from the **4s sub-shell BEFORE** they are removed from the **3d sub-shell**.
    *   **Example: Iron(II) Ion (Fe²⁺)**
        1.  Start with the neutral atom: Fe (Z=26) → `[Ar] 4s² 3d⁶`
        2.  Remove 2 electrons from the **4s** orbital first.
        3.  Fe²⁺ ion configuration: `[Ar] 3d⁶`
    *   **Example: Vanadium(III) Ion (V³⁺)**
        1.  Start with the neutral atom: V (Z=23) → `[Ar] 4s² 3d³`
        2.  Remove the two **4s** electrons, then remove one **3d** electron.
        3.  V³⁺ ion configuration: `[Ar] 3d²`
