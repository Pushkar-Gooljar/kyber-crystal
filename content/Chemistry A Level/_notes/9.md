## 9.1 Periodicity of Physical Properties (Period 3)

### 1. Atomic & Ionic Radius
[Diagram showing circles representing atoms decreasing in size from Na to Cl, and a similar trend for cations and anions]

**Trend in Atomic Radius:**
- **Description:** Decreases across the period from Na to Cl.
- **Explanation:**
    - **Nuclear charge** increases across the period (more protons).
    - Electrons are added to the same principal shell ($n=3$).
    - Shielding by inner electrons remains constant.
    - The **effective nuclear charge** increases, pulling the valence electrons more strongly towards the nucleus.

**Trend in Ionic Radius:**
This is two separate trends:
- **Cations ($Na^{+}$, $Mg^{2+}$, $Al^{3+}$):**
    - **Description:** Radius decreases significantly.
    - **Explanation:** These ions are **isoelectronic** (same electron configuration). The nuclear charge increases while the number of electrons (10) and shells (2) is constant. The stronger pull from the nucleus shrinks the ion.
- **Anions ($P^{3-}$, $S^{2-}$, $Cl^{-}$):**
    - **Description:** Radius decreases.
    - **Explanation:** These ions are also **isoelectronic**. The nuclear charge increases from P to Cl, pulling the same number of electrons (18) more tightly.
- **The Jump:** A large increase in radius occurs from $Al^{3+}$ to $P^{3-}$ because the anions have an extra electron shell.

### 2. Melting Point & Electrical Conductivity
[Graph showing melting points across Period 3: Na, Mg, Al increasing, peaking at Si, then dropping sharply for P, S, Cl, Ar]

The trends are explained by the change in structure and bonding across the period.

| Element | Structure | Bonding | **Explanation for Melting Point** | **Explanation for Electrical Conductivity** |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Na, Mg, Al** | Giant Metallic | Metallic | **Increases from Na → Al.** Strength of metallic bond increases due to more delocalised electrons per atom (1, 2, 3) and a higher ionic charge ($Na^{+}$, $Mg^{2+}$, $Al^{3+}$), leading to stronger electrostatic attraction. | **Increases from Na → Al.** Conductivity requires mobile charge carriers. The number of delocalised electrons increases. |
| **Si** | Giant Molecular | Covalent | **Highest in the period.** A very large amount of energy is needed to break the numerous strong covalent bonds throughout the giant lattice. | **Semiconductor.** No delocalised electrons. Electrons are fixed in covalent bonds but can be promoted to conduct at higher temperatures. |
| **P, S, Cl** | Simple Molecular | Covalent (within molecules), van der Waals' (between molecules) | **Very low.** Only weak intermolecular van der Waals' forces are broken. Trend is **$S_8 > P_4 > Cl_2$** as VdW forces increase with the number of electrons per molecule. | **Non-conductors.** No mobile charge carriers. Electrons are localised in covalent bonds and cannot move between molecules. |
| **Ar** | Simple Atomic | van der Waals' | **Extremely low.** Exists as individual atoms with only very weak van der Waals' forces between them. | **Non-conductor.** No mobile ions or delocalised electrons. |

### 2. Oxidation Numbers of Oxides and Chlorides
- **Trend:** The maximum oxidation number increases across the period.
- **Explanation:** It corresponds to the number of outer shell (valence) electrons.
    - Na (Group 1) → **+1** (in $Na_2O$, $NaCl$)
    - Mg (Group 2) → **+2** (in $MgO$, $MgCl_2$)
    - Al (Group 13) → **+3** (in $Al_2O_3$, $AlCl_3$)
    - Si (Group 14) → **+4** (in $SiO_2$, $SiCl_4$)
    - P (Group 15) → **+5** (in $P_4O_{10}$, $PCl_5$)
    - S (Group 16) → **+4** (in $SO_2$) or **+6** (in $SO_3$)

### 3 & 4. Reactions of Oxides (Acid/Base Behaviour)

The trend across the period is from **Basic → Amphoteric → Acidic**.

| Oxide | Nature | Reaction with Water & Equation | pH | Reaction with Acid / Base (Equations) |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **$Na_2O$** | **Strongly Basic** | Dissolves readily: $Na_2O + H_2O \rightarrow 2NaOH$ | **13-14** | Reacts with acids: $Na_2O + 2HCl \rightarrow 2NaCl + H_2O$ |
| **$MgO$** | **Weakly Basic** | Sparingly soluble: $MgO + H_2O \rightleftharpoons Mg(OH)_2$ | **9-10** | Reacts with acids: $MgO + 2HCl \rightarrow MgCl_2 + H_2O$ |
| **$Al_2O_3$** | **Amphoteric** | Insoluble. | **~7** | Reacts with **both** acids and bases: <br> Acid: $Al_2O_3 + 6HCl \rightarrow 2AlCl_3 + 3H_2O$ <br> Base: $Al_2O_3 + 2NaOH + 3H_2O \rightarrow 2NaAl(OH)_4$ |
| **$SiO_2$** | **Acidic** | Insoluble. | **~7** | Reacts only with strong, hot, concentrated bases: <br> $SiO_2 + 2NaOH \rightarrow Na_2SiO_3 + H_2O$ |
| **$P_4O_{10}$** | **Strongly Acidic** | Reacts vigorously: $P_4O_{10} + 6H_2O \rightarrow 4H_3PO_4$ | **1-2** | Reacts with bases: $P_4O_{10} + 12NaOH \rightarrow 4Na_3PO_4 + 6H_2O$ |
| **$SO_2, SO_3$**| **Strongly Acidic** | Dissolve: $SO_2 + H_2O \rightleftharpoons H_2SO_3$ <br> $SO_3 + H_2O \rightarrow H_2SO_4$ | **1-2** | Reacts with bases: $SO_2 + 2NaOH \rightarrow Na_2SO_3 + H_2O$ |

### 5. Reactions of Chlorides with Water

Behaviour depends on bonding. Ionic chlorides dissolve; covalent chlorides are hydrolysed.

| Chloride | Bonding | Reaction with Water & Explanation | pH | Observations | Equation(s) |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **$NaCl$** | **Ionic** | **Dissolution**. Ions are hydrated. | **7** | White solid dissolves to form a colourless solution. | $NaCl_{(s)} \rightarrow Na^{+}_{(aq)} + Cl^{-}_{(aq)}$ |
| **$MgCl_2$** | **Ionic** | **Dissolution**. The high charge density of $Mg^{2+}$ slightly polarises water, releasing some $H^{+}$. | **~6.5** | White solid dissolves to form a colourless solution. | $MgCl_{2(s)} \rightarrow Mg^{2+}_{(aq)} + 2Cl^{-}_{(aq)}$ <br> $[Mg(H_2O)_6]^{2+} \rightleftharpoons [Mg(H_2O)_5(OH)]^{+} + H^{+}$ |
| **$AlCl_3$** | Covalent* | **Hydrolysis**. Vigorous reaction. The small, highly charged $Al^{3+}$ ion strongly polarises water, releasing $H^{+}$. | **1-3** | White solid dissolves, steamy/misty white fumes ($HCl$) produced. White precipitate of $Al(OH)_3$ may form. | $AlCl_3 + 6H_2O \rightarrow [Al(H_2O)_6]^{3+} + 3Cl^{-}$ <br> $[Al(H_2O)_6]^{3+} \rightleftharpoons [Al(H_2O)_5(OH)]^{2+} + H^{+}$ |
| **$SiCl_4$** | **Covalent** | **Hydrolysis**. Vigorous reaction. Water acts as a nucleophile on the Si atom. | **1-2** | Steamy/misty white fumes ($HCl$) and a white precipitate ($SiO_2$) form. | $SiCl_{4(l)} + 2H_2O_{(l)} \rightarrow SiO_{2(s)} + 4HCl_{(aq)}$ |
| **$PCl_5$** | **Covalent** | **Hydrolysis**. Vigorous reaction. | **1-2** | White solid reacts, steamy/misty white fumes ($HCl$) produced. A colourless solution forms. | $PCl_{5(s)} + 4H_2O_{(l)} \rightarrow H_3PO_{4(aq)} + 5HCl_{(aq)}$ |
*\*Note: Solid AlCl₃ is ionic, but has significant covalent character.*

### 6 & 7. Explaining Trends & Deducing Bonding
- **Underlying Reason for Trends:** The key is the change in **electronegativity difference** between the Period 3 element and oxygen/chlorine.
    - **Large ΔEN (Na → Al):** Tends towards **Ionic bonding**.
    - **Small ΔEN (Si → Cl):** Tends towards **Covalent bonding**.
- **Deducing Bonding from Properties:**
    - **Giant Ionic:** High m.p., conducts when molten, soluble in water (pH ≥ 6.5). Ex: $MgO, NaCl$.
    - **Giant Molecular:** Very high m.p., insoluble, non-conductor. Ex: $SiO_2$.
    - **Simple Molecular:** Low m.p./b.p., non-conductor, often hydrolysed vigorously by water to give acidic solutions (pH 1-3) with misty fumes. Ex: $SiCl_4, P_4O_{10}$.

## 9.3 Chemical Periodicity of Other Elements

### 1. Predicting Properties
- **Down a Group:** Properties are similar. Germanium (Ge, Period 4, Group 14) will be similar to Silicon (Si).
    - **Oxide ($GeO_2$):** Acidic, giant molecular structure.
    - **Chloride ($GeCl_4$):** Covalent liquid, hydrolyses in water.
- **Across a Period:** The trends from Basic → Amphoteric → Acidic for oxides, and Ionic → Covalent for chlorides, are generally repeated.

### 2. Deducing Identity from Properties
Use the unique combination of physical and chemical properties to identify an element.

> [!example] Example Deduction
> **Clue:** "Element Z forms a white solid oxide with a very high melting point. The oxide is insoluble in water but reacts with both HCl and NaOH. The chloride of Z has formula $ZCl_2$ and forms a solution with a pH of ~6.5."
> **Deduction:**
> - High m.p. oxide → Giant structure.
> - Oxide reacts with acid and base → **Amphoteric** (like Al) OR **Basic** (like Mg, Na).
> - Chloride $ZCl_2$ → **Group 2** element.
> - Chloride solution pH ~6.5 → Characteristic of $MgCl_2$.
> - Combining these: a Group 2 element with a basic (not amphoteric) oxide is needed.
> - **Conclusion:** Element Z is **Magnesium**.
