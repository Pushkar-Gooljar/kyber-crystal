>[!info] Core Concept: Covalent Bonds as Orbital Overlap
>- A covalent bond is formed when atomic orbitals from two different atoms **overlap** in space, creating a region where a shared pair of electrons can exist.
>- The extent of this orbital overlap determines the bond strength - **greater overlap leads to stronger bonds.**
>

## The Two Types of Covalent Bonds: Sigma (σ) and Pi (π)
The geometry of how orbitals overlap results in two distinct types of covalent bonds.

### 1. Sigma (σ) Bonds
A sigma bond is formed by the **direct, head-on overlap** of atomic orbitals between the bonding atoms.
- **Key Feature:** The electron density is concentrated and symmetrically distributed **directly between the two bonding nuclei**, along the internuclear axis.
- **Formation:** Can be formed by from the overlap of:
	- `s-s` orbitals 
	- `s-p` orbitals 
	- `p-p` orbitals (head-on)
		- Hybridised orbitals (e.g. `sp³-s` or `sp²-sp²`)
- **Strength:** Sigma bonds are **strong** due to the high degree of direct orbital overlap.

>[!tip] Rule of Thumb
>- All **single bonds** are sigma bonds.
>- The **first bond** in any multiple bond (double or triple) is always a sigma bond.


### 2. Pi (π) Bonds

A pi bond is formed by the **sideways overlap** of adjacent $\mathrm{p}$-orbitals above and below the σ bond

- **Key Features:** The electron density is concentrated in two regions, **above and below** the plane of the sigma bond. The internuclear axis itself has zero electron density (it is a nodal plane)
- **Formation:** A π bond is *only* formed by the sideways overlap of **unhybridised p-orbitals**.
- **Strength:** Pi bonds are **weaker** than sigma bonds because the sideways overlap is less effective (less direct) that head-on overlap

>[!tip] Rule of Thumb
>- A **double bond** consists of `1 σ bond` and `1 π bond`.
>- A **triple bond** consists of `1 σ bond` and `2 π bonds` (oriented at 90° to each other).

### Similarities between Sigma (σ) and Pi (π) Bonds

This table highlights the fundamental characteristics that both types of bonds share as covalent bonds.

| Feature                  | Description                                                                                                                             |
| :----------------------- | :-------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Type of Bond**         | Both are types of **covalent bonds** that hold atoms together within a molecule.                                                        |
| **Formation Mechanism**  | Both are formed by the **overlap of atomic orbitals**.                                                                                  |
| **Number of Electrons**  | Both consist of one **shared pair of electrons** (2 electrons total).                                                                   |
| **Nature of Attraction** | Both result from the **electrostatic attraction** between the shared pair of electrons and the positive nuclei of the two bonded atoms. |

### Differences between Sigma (σ) and Pi (π) Bonds

This table provides a detailed comparison of the distinct features of sigma and pi bonds.

| Feature | Sigma (σ) Bond | Pi (π) Bond |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Orbital Overlap** | Formed by **direct, head-on** overlap of orbitals. | Formed by **sideways, parallel** overlap of p-orbitals. |
| **Shape of Electron Cloud** | **Symmetrical** and cylindrical around the internuclear axis. Electron density is concentrated *between* the nuclei. | **Not symmetrical**. Electron density is concentrated in two regions, *above and below* the internuclear axis. |
| **Relative Bond Strength** | **Stronger**. The direct overlap is more effective and extensive. | **Weaker**. The sideways overlap is less effective. |
| **Rotation Around Bond Axis**| **Free rotation is possible** without breaking the bond. | **Rotation is restricted**. Attempting to rotate would break the sideways p-orbital overlap. |
| **Occurrence in Bonds** | **Always present**. The first bond in any covalent link is a σ bond. All single bonds are σ bonds. | **Only present in multiple bonds**. The second bond in a double bond and the second/third bonds in a triple bond are π bonds. |
| **Types of Orbitals Involved**| Can be formed from `s`, `p`, and **hybridised orbitals** (sp, sp², sp³). | Can *only* be formed from **unhybridised p-orbitals**. |

## The Concept of Hybridisation
>[!info] Definition: Hybridisation
>- Hybridisation is the mixing of atomic orbitals within the same atom
>- to produce a set of degenerate (same energy) hybrid orbitals
>- that have characteristics of the original orbitals
>- and are better arranged in space for covalent bonding
>- ==The number of hybrid orbitals formed is equal to the number of orbitals mixed==
>	- 1 s + 1 p = 2 $\mathrm{sp}$
>	- 1s + 2 p = 3 $\mathrm{sp^2}$
>	- 1s + 3p = 4 $\mathrm{sp^3}$



| Hybridisation | Recipe (Mixing of Orbitals) |               Resulting Orbitals                |    Geometry & Bond Angle    |    Bonding Formed     | Charge Clouds on central atom |
| :-----------: | :-------------------------: | :---------------------------------------------: | :-------------------------: | :-------------------: | :---------------------------: |
|    **sp³**    |          1 s + 3 p          |           4 identical `sp³` orbitals            |  **Tetrahedral** (~109.5°)  |  4 single (σ) bonds   |               4               |
|    **sp²**    |          1 s + 2 p          | 3 identical `sp²` orbitals + `1 unhybridised p` | **Trigonal Planar** (~120°) | 3 σ bonds + 1 π bond  |               3               |
|    **sp**     |          1 s + 1 p          | 2 identical `sp` orbitals + `2 unhybridised p`  |     **Linear** (~180°)      | 2 σ bonds + 2 π bonds |               2               |
