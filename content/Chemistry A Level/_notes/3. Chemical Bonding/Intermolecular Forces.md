## ⚡ Electronegativity and Bond Polarity

>[!info] Definition: Electronegativity
>Electronegativity is the ability of an atom to **attract the shared pair of electrons** towards itself in a covalent bond.
>
>- **Periodic Trend**: Electronegativity `==increases across a period==` and `==decreases down a group==`.
>- **Most Electronegative Elements**: **Fluorine (F)**, **Oxygen (O)**, and **Nitrogen (N)** are the most electronegative elements.

### From Electronegativity to Bond Polarity

The difference in electronegativity ($\Delta EN$) between two bonded atoms determines the polarity of a covalent bond.

| Type of Bond | $\Delta EN$ | Description | Example |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Non-Polar Covalent** | **Zero** | The bonding electrons are shared **equally** between the two atoms. This occurs when the atoms are identical. | $\ce{Cl-Cl}$ in $\ce{Cl2}$ |
| **Polar Covalent** | **Non-zero** | The bonding electrons are shared **unequally**. The electron cloud is distorted towards the more electronegative atom. | $\ce{H-Cl}$ |

In a polar bond:
-   The **more electronegative** atom gains a **partial negative charge (δ⁻)**.
-   The **less electronegative** atom gains a **partial positive charge (δ⁺)**.
-   This separation of charge creates a **bond dipole**. The larger the $\Delta EN$, the more polar the bond.

## 🔬 Molecular Polarity & Dipole Moments

A **dipole moment** is a measure of the overall polarity of a molecule. It depends on two factors:
1.  The presence of polar bonds.
2.  The **geometry** of the molecule.

>[!tip] Polar vs. Non-Polar Molecules
>- A molecule with polar bonds will be **polar** if its geometry is **asymmetrical**, causing the individual bond dipoles to add up to a **net dipole moment**.
>- A molecule with polar bonds will be **non-polar** if its geometry is **symmetrical**, causing the individual bond dipoles to **cancel each other out**.

| Molecule | Polar Bonds Present? | Shape | Bond Dipoles Cancel? | Overall Polarity |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **$\ce{CO2}$** | Yes (C=O is polar) | Linear | Yes | **Non-Polar** |
| **$\ce{CCl4}$** | Yes (C-Cl is polar) | Tetrahedral | Yes | **Non-Polar** |
| **$\ce{H2O}$** | Yes (O-H is polar) | Bent | No | **Polar** |
| **$\ce{NH3}$** | Yes (N-H is polar) | Trigonal Pyramidal | No | **Polar** |

## 🤝 Intermolecular Forces (van der Waals' Forces)

>[!abstract] Intramolecular vs. Intermolecular Forces
>- **Intramolecular forces** are the strong forces *within* a molecule (covalent, ionic, metallic bonds).
>- **Intermolecular forces (IMFs)** are the much weaker attractive forces *between* molecules.
>- `==Intramolecular bonds are much stronger than intermolecular forces==`.

**Van der Waals' forces** is the generic term for all intermolecular forces. They are responsible for the physical properties of simple molecular substances (like boiling points).

### Types of van der Waals' Forces (in order of increasing strength)

#### 1. Instantaneous Dipole–Induced Dipole (id-id) Forces
-   Also known as **London Dispersion Forces**.
-   `==The weakest type of IMF, but present between ALL molecules==`.
-   **How it works**:
    1.  The electron cloud in an atom/molecule is in constant motion.
    2.  At any given instant, the electron distribution can be uneven, creating a temporary, **instantaneous dipole**.
    3.  This temporary dipole can **induce** a dipole in a neighbouring molecule, leading to a weak, fleeting attraction.
-   **Strength depends on**:
    -   **Number of electrons**: More electrons lead to a larger, more polarisable electron cloud, resulting in stronger id-id forces. (This is why boiling points increase down Group 17 and for larger alkanes).

#### 2. Permanent Dipole–Permanent Dipole (pd-pd) Forces
-   Occur `==only between polar molecules==`.
-   **How it works**: The δ⁺ end of one polar molecule is electrostatically attracted to the δ⁻ end of a neighbouring polar molecule.
-   **Strength**: Stronger than id-id forces for molecules of similar size/number of electrons.

#### 3. Hydrogen Bonding
-   `==The strongest type of intermolecular force==`.
-   It is a special, very strong case of a **permanent dipole–permanent dipole interaction**.

>[!warning] Conditions for Hydrogen Bonding
>1.  A molecule must contain a hydrogen atom covalently bonded to a very electronegative atom: **Nitrogen (N)**, **Oxygen (O)**, or **Fluorine (F)**.
>2.  A second molecule must have a **lone pair of electrons** on its N, O, or F atom.

-   **How it works**: The H atom in an N-H, O-H, or F-H bond is so electron deficient that it carries a large partial positive charge (δ⁺). This highly charged H is strongly attracted to the lone pair on a N, O, or F atom of a neighbouring molecule.

## 💧 Anomalous Properties of Water

The ability of water ($\ce{H2O}$) to form extensive hydrogen bonds gives it unique, life-sustaining properties.

1.  **High Melting and Boiling Points**: Water has a much higher boiling point (100°C) than other Group 16 hydrides (e.g., H₂S, -60°C). A large amount of energy is needed to overcome the strong hydrogen bonds between water molecules, in addition to the London dispersion forces.
2.  **Ice is Less Dense than Liquid Water**: In liquid water, molecules are close but randomly arranged. In ice, the molecules arrange into an **open, rigid, tetrahedral lattice** to maximise the hydrogen bonding. This "open" structure makes solid ice less dense than liquid water, which is why ice floats.
3.  **High Surface Tension**: Hydrogen bonds create a strong cohesive force between water molecules. At the surface, this results in a net inward pull, creating a "skin" that resists being broken.
