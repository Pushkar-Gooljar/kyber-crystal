## Cations (Positive ions)
- Metals tend to lose their valance electrons to be left of a completely filled outer shell.
- Losing negatively charged electrons results in the formation of a positively charged ion called a cation.
- Example: Sodium ($\mathrm{Na}$)
	- Neutral atom: $\mathrm{Na}$ has the electronic configuration $\mathrm{1s^22s^22p^63s^1}$
	- To achieve a stable octet, it loses its valence electron (the single $\mathrm{3s}$ electron.)
	- Cation: $\ce{Na+}$ has the electronic configuration $\mathrm{1s^22s^22p^6}$ (same as neon)

## Anions (Negative ions)
- Non-metals tend to gain electrons to complete to complete their valence shell.
- Gaining negatively charged electrons results in the formation of a negatively charged ion called an anion.
- Example: Chlorine $\mathrm{Cl}$
	- Neutral atom: $\mathrm{Cl}$ has the electronic configuration $\mathrm{1s^22s^22p^63s^23p^5}$
	- To achieve a stable octet, it gains one electron into its $\mathrm{3p}$ subshell.
	- Anion: $\ce{Cl-}$ has the configuration $\mathrm{1s^22s^22p^63s^23p^6}$ (same as argon).

## Transfer of electrons
- In ionic bonding, electrons lost by the metal atom are transferred to the non-metal atom. 
- This transfer allows both atoms to achieve full outer electron shells, resulting in the formation of stable ions having noble gas electronic configuration.

## Predicting ion charges
### Main group elements
- Elements in the same group of the periodic table form ions with the same charge as they have the same number of outer shell electrons:
	- Group 1: Lose 1 $\ce{e-}$ $\to$ `+1` 
	- Group 2: Lose 2 $\ce{e-}$ $\to$ `+2` 
	- Group 13: Lose 3 $\ce{e-}$ $\to$ `+3` 
	- Group 15: Lose 3 $\ce{e-}$ $\to$ `-3` 
	- Group 16: Lose 2 $\ce{e-}$ $\to$ `-2` 
	- Group 17: Lose 1 $\ce{e-}$ $\to$ `-1`

### Transition elements
- Unlike main group elements, transition elements have partially filled $\mathrm{d}$ subshells.
- Electrons are removed from the $\mathrm{4s}$ subshell and then the $\mathrm{3d}$ subshell.
- Since the energies of the $\mathrm{4s}$ and $\mathrm{3d}$ subshells are very close, transition metals can form ions with variable charges.
- For example, iron can form both $\ce{Fe^{2+}}$ and $\ce{Fe^{3+}}$ ions.
