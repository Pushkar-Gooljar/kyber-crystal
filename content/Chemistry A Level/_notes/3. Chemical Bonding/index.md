>[!info] Definition: Covalent Bonding
>A covalent bond is the strong **electrostatic attraction** between the **positive nuclei** of two atoms and a **shared pair of electrons** between them.
>
>This sharing allows atoms, typically non-metals, to achieve a stable outer electron shell, usually a full octet (8 electrons), similar to a noble gas.

---

## 🔬 Representing Covalent Bonds

We use **dot-and-cross diagrams** (Lewis structures) to show how valence electrons are shared.
-   **Single Bond**: One shared pair of electrons (2 electrons total). Represented by a single line (e.g., `H-H`).
-   **Double Bond**: Two shared pairs of electrons (4 electrons total). Represented by two lines (e.g., `O=O`).
-   **Triple Bond**: Three shared pairs of electrons (6 electrons total). Represented by three lines (e.g., `N≡N`).

>[!warning] Expanded Octet
>Elements in **Period 3 and below** (e.g., P, S, Cl) can have more than 8 electrons in their outer shell. This is because they have empty, energetically accessible **d-orbitals** that can accommodate extra electrons.

## 🤝 Coordinate (Dative Covalent) Bonding

>[!info] Definition: Coordinate Bond
>A coordinate bond is a covalent bond in which `==one atom provides both electrons==` to the shared pair.
>- **Requirements**: An atom with a **lone pair** of electrons (a Lewis Base) and an atom with an empty, accessible **orbital** (a Lewis Acid).
>- It is represented by an arrow (`→`) pointing from the donor atom to the acceptor atom. Once formed, it is identical to a normal covalent bond.

#### Example 1: The Ammonium Ion (NH₄⁺)
-   Ammonia (`NH₃`) has a **lone pair** of electrons on the nitrogen atom.
-   A hydrogen ion (`H⁺`) has an **empty 1s orbital**.
-   The lone pair from the nitrogen is donated to the empty orbital of the `H⁺` ion, forming a coordinate bond.
<img src="https://www.chem.ucla.edu/~harding/IGOC/A/ammonium_ion_formation.png" class="custom-img" style="max-height: 150px;">

#### Example 2: The Aluminium Chloride Dimer (Al₂Cl₆)
-   In the gas phase at lower temperatures, Aluminium Chloride exists as a dimer (`Al₂Cl₆`).
-   A single `AlCl₃` molecule is **electron deficient**, as the central Al only has 6 valence electrons.
-   To achieve a more stable octet, a chlorine atom from one `AlCl₃` molecule uses one of its **lone pairs** to form a coordinate bond with the empty orbital on the aluminium atom of a neighbouring `AlCl₃` molecule. This happens twice to form the dimer.
