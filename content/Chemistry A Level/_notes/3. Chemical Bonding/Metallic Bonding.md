> [!info] Definition
> - Metallic bonding is the strong electrostatic attraction between positive ions in a giant metallic lattice and a 'sea' of delocalised electrons.

## 💎 Structure of metallic bonding
- The structure of a metal can be visualised as a **giant metallic lattice**

- **Lattice of cations:** 
	- Metal atoms readily lose their outer shell (valence) electrons to become positively charged ions. 
	- These cations are arranged in a regular, repeating, and closely packed three-dimensional structure.
- **'Sea' of delocalised electrons:** 
	- The lost valence electrons are no longer associated with any single atom.
	- They become delocalised, meaning they are free to move throughout the entire metallic structure, forming a mobile 'sea' of negative charge that holds the positive ions together.


## ✨ Properties of metals

The properties of ionic compounds result from their lattice structure.
1. **High electrical and thermal conductivity**
	- The delocalised electrons act as charge carriers.
	- When a voltage is applied, they can flow freely through the lattice, creating an electric current.
	- Similarly, these free electrons can rapidly transfer kinetic energy  (free electron diffusion) throughout the structure, making metals excellent thermal conductors.
2. **High melting and boiling points**
	- A large amount of energy is required to overcome the strong electrostatic forces of attraction between the positive cations and the 'sea' of delocalised electrons. 
	- This results in most metals being solid at room temperature with high melting points.

## ⚖️ Factors Influencing the Strength of Metallic Bonding
A stronger metallic bond results in a higher melting point, greater density, and increased hardness. The strength depends on:
1. **Number of Delocalised Electrons**: More delocalised electrons per atom increases the charge density of the "electron sea," leading to a stronger attraction.
2. **Charge of the Metal Cation**: A greater charge on the cation (e.g., Mg²⁺ vs. Na⁺) results in a stronger electrostatic attraction to the delocalised electrons.
3. **Ionic Radius**: A smaller ionic radius means the delocalised electrons are closer to the positive nucleus, resulting in a stronger attraction and a more tightly packed lattice.

>[!example]
>Explain the trend in melting points: Sodium (98 °C), Magnesium (650 °C), Aluminium (660 °C).
>
>Answer:
>- Moving from `Na` to `Mg` to `Al` across Period 3:
>  - The number of delocalised valence electrons increases from **1** (`Na`) to **2** (`Mg`) to **3** (`Al`).
>  - The charge on the cation increases from **+1** (`Na⁺`) to **+2** (`Mg²⁺`) to **+3** (`Al³⁺`).
>  - The ionic radius decreases.
>- Both the increasing number of delocalised electrons and the increasing cationic charge lead to a much stronger electrostatic attraction between the ions and the electron sea.
>- Therefore, more energy is required to overcome these forces, resulting in a significant increase in melting point from Na to Mg to Al.
