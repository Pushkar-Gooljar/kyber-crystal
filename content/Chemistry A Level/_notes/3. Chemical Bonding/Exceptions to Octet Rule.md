>[!info] The Octet Rule
>- The octet rule states that atoms tend to form chemical bonds to achieve a stable electron configuration with eight electrons in their outer shell.
>- However there are some notable exceptions

## 1. Incomplete Octet (Less than 8 Electrons)
- Certain elements, particularly  those in Group 2 and Group 13 (like beryllium, boron and aluminium), can be stable with fewer than eight electrons.
- These molecules are called **electron deficient**.

**Example: Boron Trifluoride ($\ce{BF_{3}}$):**
- Boron (Group 13) has 3 valence electrons.
- It forms 3 single covalent bonds with three fluorine atoms.
- The central boron atom in $\ce{BF_{3}}$ surrounded by only **6 electrons** (3 bonding pairs).

## 2. Expanded Octet (More than 8 Electrons)
> [!tip] Key Requirement  
> - An expanded octet is only possible for non-metal atoms in **Period 3 and below**
>-  Examples: $\mathrm{P}$, $\mathrm{S}$, $\mathrm{Cl}$, $\mathrm{Br}$

- These elements can accommodate more than 8 electrons in their valence shell. 
- This is because they have energetically accessible but empty $\mathrm{d}$-orbitals, which can be used to hold additional electron pairs.
