### 11.1 Physical Properties of Group 17 Elements ($Cl_2, Br_2, I_2$)

#### 11.1.1 & 11.1.3: Colour & Volatility

| Element | State (RTP) | Colour | Trend in Volatility |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Chlorine ($Cl_2$)** | Gas | Pale yellow-green | **Most volatile** |
| **Bromine ($Br_2$)** | Liquid | Dark red-brown | ↓ |
| **Iodine ($I_2$)** | Solid | Grey-black solid, purple vapour | **Least volatile** |

> [!info] Explanation of Volatility Trend
> - **Volatility decreases down the group** (Boiling point increases).
> - All halogens are **simple molecular** substances ($X_2$).
> - The only forces between their non-polar molecules are **instantaneous dipole-induced dipole forces (van der Waals')**.
> - As you go down the group, the number of electrons per molecule **increases**.
> - This leads to **stronger van der Waals' forces** of attraction between the molecules.
> - More energy is required to overcome these stronger intermolecular forces, hence the boiling point increases and volatility decreases.

#### 11.1.2: Bond Strength (X-X Bond Energy)

-   **Trend:** The bond energy of the X-X covalent bond **decreases** down the group from chlorine to iodine.
    -   $Cl-Cl$ (242 kJ/mol) > $Br-Br$ (193 kJ/mol) > $I-I$ (151 kJ/mol)
-   **Explanation:**
    -   As you go down the group, the **atomic radius increases**.
    -   The shared pair of electrons in the covalent bond is progressively **further from the nuclei**.
    -   The electrostatic attraction between the nuclei and the bonding pair of electrons **weakens**.
    -   Less energy is required to break the bond.
-   **Anomaly:** Fluorine ($F_2$, 158 kJ/mol) has an unexpectedly weak bond. This is due to its very small size, which causes strong repulsion between the lone pairs of electrons on the adjacent atoms, weakening the covalent bond. (This is A2-level detail, not always required but good to know).

### 11.2 Halogen Elements & Hydrogen Halides

#### 11.2.1: Reactivity as Oxidising Agents

-   **Trend:** The reactivity of halogens as **oxidising agents decreases** down the group.
    -   $Cl_2 > Br_2 > I_2$
-   **Explanation:** Halogens react by gaining an electron (they are reduced). Their ability to do this depends on how strongly they attract an incoming electron.
    -   Down the group, **atomic radius increases** and **electron shielding increases**.
    -   This leads to a weaker attraction between the nucleus and an external electron.
    -   Therefore, they become less powerful oxidising agents.
-   **Displacement Reactions:** A more reactive halogen will displace the halide ion of a less reactive halogen from its solution.
    -   **Ionic Equation:** $$Cl_{2(aq)} + 2Br^{-}_{(aq)} \rightarrow 2Cl^{-}_{(aq)} + Br_{2(aq)}$$ (Solution turns orange/brown)
    -   **Ionic Equation:** $$Br_{2(aq)} + 2I^{-}_{(aq)} \rightarrow 2Br^{-}_{(aq)} + I_{2(aq)}$$ (Solution turns brown/black)

#### 11.2.2: Reactions with Hydrogen

-   **Reaction:** The halogens react with hydrogen to form hydrogen halides (HX). $$H_{2(g)} + X_{2(g)} \rightarrow 2HX_{(g)}$$
-   **Trend in Reactivity:** The vigour of the reaction **decreases** down the group, mirroring their reactivity as oxidising agents.
    -   **$H_2 + Cl_2$:** Explosive reaction in UV light.
    -   **$H_2 + Br_2$:** Reacts on heating.
    -   **$H_2 + I_2$:** Forms a reversible equilibrium on heating.

#### 11.2.3: Thermal Stability of Hydrogen Halides (HX)

-   **Trend:** The thermal stability of the hydrogen halides **decreases** down the group.
    -   $HF > HCl > HBr > HI$
-   **Explanation:**
    -   This is the reverse of the X-X bond strength trend. It depends on the **H-X bond strength**.
    -   Down the group, the halogen atom ($X$) becomes larger.
    -   The H-X bond length **increases**, and the orbital overlap becomes less effective.
    -   The **H-X bond becomes weaker** and requires less energy to break.
    -   Therefore, HI decomposes into H₂ and I₂ at a much lower temperature than HCl does.

### 11.3 Reactions of Halide Ions ($Cl^−, Br^−, I^−$)

#### 11.3.1: Reactivity as Reducing Agents

-   **Trend:** The ability of halide ions to act as **reducing agents increases** down the group.
    -   $I^− > Br^− > Cl^−$
-   **Explanation:** Halide ions react by losing an electron (they are oxidised).
    -   Down the group, the **ionic radius increases** and the **outermost electron is further from the nucleus** and more shielded.
    -   This electron is held less tightly and is **easier to lose**.
    -   Therefore, iodide ions are the strongest reducing agents.

#### 11.3.2: Reactions with Reagents

**(a) With Aqueous Silver Nitrate, $AgNO_3(aq)$, followed by Ammonia**

This is the standard test to distinguish between halide ions.

| Halide Ion | Reagent: $AgNO_3(aq)$ | Equation | Reagent: $NH_3(aq)$ |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **$Cl^−$** | **White** precipitate | $Ag^{+}_{(aq)} + Cl^{-}_{(aq)} \rightarrow AgCl_{(s)}$ | Precipitate **dissolves** in **dilute** $NH_3(aq)$ |
| **$Br^−$** | **Cream** precipitate | $Ag^{+}_{(aq)} + Br^{-}_{(aq)} \rightarrow AgBr_{(s)}$ | Precipitate **dissolves** only in **concentrated** $NH_3(aq)$ |
| **$I^−$** | **Yellow** precipitate | $Ag^{+}_{(aq)} + I^{-}_{(aq)} \rightarrow AgI_{(s)}$ | Precipitate is **insoluble** in both dilute and concentrated $NH_3(aq)$ |

**(b) With Concentrated Sulfuric Acid, $H_2SO_4(conc)$**

This reaction powerfully demonstrates the increasing reducing power of the halide ions.

| Halide Ion | Role of $H_2SO_4$ | Observations | Equation(s) |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **$Cl^−$** | **Only an acid.** Not a strong enough reducing agent to reduce the sulfur. | **Steamy/misty white fumes** of $HCl$ gas. | $$NaCl_{(s)} + H_2SO_4{(l)} \rightarrow NaHSO_4{(s)} + HCl_{(g)}$$ *(Acid-base reaction)* |
| **$Br^−$** | **Acid, then Oxidising Agent.** Stronger reducing agent reduces sulfur from +6 to +4. | **Steamy white fumes** ($HBr$), **brown/orange fumes** ($Br_2$), and a colourless, choking gas ($SO_2$). | 1. **Acid-base:** $NaBr + H_2SO_4 \rightarrow NaHSO_4 + HBr$ <br> 2. **Redox:** $2HBr + H_2SO_4 \rightarrow Br_2 + SO_2 + 2H_2O$ |
| **$I^−$** | **Acid, then strong Oxidising Agent.** Strongest reducing agent reduces sulfur from +6 to +4, +0, and -2. | **Steamy white fumes** ($HI$), **purple vapour / grey-black solid** ($I_2$), colourless choking gas ($SO_2$), **yellow solid** (S), and a gas with a **smell of rotten eggs** ($H_2S$). | 1. **Acid-base:** $NaI + H_2SO_4 \rightarrow NaHSO_4 + HI$ <br> 2. **Redox:** $2HI + H_2SO_4 \rightarrow I_2 + SO_2 + 2H_2O$ <br> (and/or) $6HI + H_2SO_4 \rightarrow 3I_2 + S + 4H_2O$ <br> (and/or) $8HI + H_2SO_4 \rightarrow 4I_2 + H_2S + 4H_2O$ |

### 11.4 The Reactions of Chlorine

#### 11.4.2: Use in Water Purification

-   **Process:** Chlorine is added to water in small amounts.
-   **Reaction:** It undergoes a **disproportionation** reaction with the water.
    $$Cl_2{(g)} + H_2O_{(l)} \rightleftharpoons HCl_{(aq)} + HOCl_{(aq)}$$
-   **Explanation:**
    -   **Chloric(I) acid ($HOCl$)** and the chlorate(I) ion ($ClO^−$) are the active species.
    -   They are powerful oxidising agents that **kill bacteria and microbes**, making the water safe to drink.

#### 11.4.1: Reaction with Sodium Hydroxide (Disproportionation)

**Disproportionation:** A reaction where an element is simultaneously oxidised and reduced.

-   **With Cold, Dilute NaOH:**
    -   **Equation:** $$Cl_2 + 2NaOH_{(aq)} \rightarrow NaCl_{(aq)} + NaClO_{(aq)} + H_2O_{(l)}$$
    -   **Oxidation Numbers:** Chlorine ($0$) is reduced to $Cl^−$ (**-1**) and oxidised to $ClO^−$ (**+1**).
    -   **Products:** Sodium chloride and **sodium chlorate(I)** (bleach).

-   **With Hot, Concentrated NaOH:**
    -   **Equation:** $$3Cl_2 + 6NaOH_{(aq)} \rightarrow 5NaCl_{(aq)} + NaClO_{3(aq)} + 3H_2O_{(l)}$$
    -   **Oxidation Numbers:** Chlorine ($0$) is reduced to $Cl^−$ (**-1**) and oxidised to $ClO_3^−$ (**+5**).
    -   **Products:** Sodium chloride and **sodium chlorate(V)**.
