# Specific Heat Capacity
- It is the energy required to raise the temperature of 1 g of a substance by 1 K (or 1 °C)
- The specific heat capacity of water is 4.18 $\mathrm{Jg^{-1} °C^{-1}}$, this means that to raise the temperature of 1 g of water by 1  °C, 4.18 J of energy is needed
## Assumptions for Solutions
Since a solution is **mostly made of water**, the following assumptions can be made:
1. Solutions have the **same density as water**, i.e. 1 $g$ of a solution occupies 1 $cm^3$. Therefore, mass can be substituted for volume in certain equations
2. Solutions have the **same specific heat capacity as water**

# Measuring Enthalpy Changes
- **Calorimetry** is a technique used to measure changes in enthalpy of chemical reactions.
- A **calorimeter** can be made up of a polystyrene cup

**The energy transferred as heat can be calculated using the following equation:**
$$
\large
q = mc\Delta T
$$
Where:
- $q$ : the heat transferred, $\mathrm{J}$
- $m$ : mass of substance (e.g. water, solution, etc), $\mathrm{g}$
- $c$ : specific heat capacity of substance, $\mathrm{Jg^{-1}°C^{-1}}$ or $\mathrm{Jg^{-1}K^{-1}}$
- $\Delta T$ : change in temperature, °C or $\mathrm{K}$  
</br>

**Mass can be substituted for volume if the density of the substance is 1.00 $\mathrm{\,gcm^{3}}$ such as in the case of water and aqueous solutions, this leads to the following equation:**
$$
\large
q = vc\Delta T
$$
Where:
- $q$ : the heat transferred, $\mathrm{J}$
- $v$ : volume of substance (e.g. water, solution), $\mathrm{cm^{3}}$
- $c$ : specific heat capacity of substance, $\mathrm{Jg^{-1}°C^{-1}}$ or $\mathrm{Jg^{-1}K^{-1}}$
- $\Delta T$ : change in temperature, °C or $\mathrm{K}$  


 
</br>

>[!worked-example]
> **In a calorimetric experiment, 2.50 $g$ of methane is burnt in excess oxygen.
> 30 % of the energy released during the combustion is absorbed by 500 g of water, causing the temperature to rise from 25 $\mathrm{°C}$ to 68 $\mathrm{°C}$.**
> 
> The specific heat capacity of water is 4.18 $\mathrm{J\,g^{-1}\,K^{-1}}$.
> Calculate the total energy released per gram of methane burnt.
> 
> **Answer**
> **Step 1:** Gather necessary values for the equation $q=mc\Delta T$
> $$
> \large
> \begin{align}
> m \space (\text{of water}) &= 500 \space g \\
> c \space (\text{of water}) &= 4.18 \space \mathrm{J\,g^{-1}\,K^{-1}} \\
> \Delta T \space (\text{of water}) &= 68 \mathrm{°C} - 25 \mathrm{°C} = 43 \mathrm{°C} = 43 \mathrm{\,K} \\ \\
> \end{align}
> $$
> **Step 2:** Replace the values in the equation and perform the calculation
> $$
> \large
> \begin{align}
> q&=mc\Delta T \\
> q&= 500 \times 4.18 \times 43 \\
> q&= 89 \space 870 \mathrm{\,J}
> \end{align}
> $$
> **Step 3:** Conversion 30% to 100%
> - The value of $\mathrm{q}$ is only 30% of the total energy released by 2.50 $\mathrm{g}$ of methane 
> $$
> \large
> \begin{align}
> 30 \% &\to 89 \space 870 \mathrm{\,J} \\
> 100 \% &\to \frac{{100 \times 89 \space 870}}{30} \\
> &= 299 \space 567 \mathrm{\,J}
> \end{align}
> $$
> 
>**Step 4:** Calculate the energy released by 1.00 $\mathrm{g}$
> - The energy calculated is released by 2.50 g of methane
> $$
> \large
> \begin{align}
> 2.50  \mathrm{\,g} &\to 299 \space 567 \mathrm{\,J} \\
> 1.00 \mathrm{\,g} &\to \frac{{1 \times 299 \space 567 }}{2.50} \\
> &= 119 \space 827 \mathrm{\,J}
> \end{align}
> $$
> 
>**Step 5:** Convert the answer from $\mathrm{J}$ to $\mathrm{kJ}$: $\large \frac{119827}{1000}=119.8=120 \mathrm{\,kJ}$ (3 s.f.)

## Molar Enthalpy Change
Based on the **First Law of Thermodynamics** (conservation of energy):
- Heat **lost** by the [[5.1.1 Enthalpy Change#^3b55f7|system]] = Heat **gained** by the  [[5.1.1 Enthalpy Change#^3b55f7|surroundings]], or
- Heat **gained** by the system = Heat **lost** by the surroundings 

**Mathematically, this is expressed as:**
$$
\Large
\begin{align}
q_{\text{system}} &= -(q_{\text{surroundings}})
\end{align}
$$
The equation $q=mc\Delta T$ is used to calculate the heat absorbed or released by the surroundings, i.e. the water/solution in the calorimeter.

As we established, $q_{\text{system}} = -(q_{\text{surroundings}})$, since the heat lost or gained by the surroundings is given by $mc\Delta T$, it follows that the heat released or absorbed by the chemical reaction itself (the system) is given by:
$$
\Large
q_{\text{system}} = -mc\Delta T
$$
This gives the total heat change for the specific amount of reactants used in the experiment.

The enthalpy change per mole ($\Delta H$) of defined reactant or product is given by:
$$
\large
\begin{align}
\Delta H &= \frac{{q_{system}}}{\text{number of moles}} \\ \\
\Delta H &= \frac{{-mc\Delta T}}{n}
\end{align}
$$
</br>

>[!worked-example]
> **In a coffee-cup calorimeter, 50.0 $\mathrm{cm^3}$ of 1.0 $\mathrm{mol\,dm^{-3}}$ hydrochloric acid is mixed with 50.0 $\mathrm{cm^3}$ of 1.0 $\mathrm{mol\,dm^{-3}}$ sodium hydroxide solution. The initial temperature of both solutions was 22.5 $\mathrm{°C}$ and the final temperature reached was 29.2 $\mathrm{°C}$.**
> 
> Calculate the standard enthalpy change of neutralisation ($\Delta H_{\text{neut}}^{\ominus}$) for the reaction.
> Assume the density of the solution is 1.0 $\mathrm{g\,cm^{-3}}$ and the specific heat capacity is 4.18 $\mathrm{J\,g^{-1}\,K^{-1}}$.
> 
> **Answer**
> The standard enthalpy of neutralisation is the enthalpy change when 1 mole of water is produced by the reaction of an acid and a base.
> 
> The balanced chemical equation is:
> $$
> \large
> \mathrm{HCl(aq) + NaOH(aq) \to NaCl(aq) + H_2O(l)}
> $$
> **Step 1:** Gather necessary values to find the heat absorbed by the solution, $q$.
> $$
> \large
> \begin{align}
> \text{Total Volume} &= 50.0 \space \mathrm{cm^3} + 50.0 \space \mathrm{cm^3} = 100.0 \space \mathrm{cm^3} \\
> m \space (\text{of solution}) &= \text{Volume} \times \text{Density} = 100.0 \space \mathrm{cm^3} \times 1.0 \space \mathrm{g\,cm^{-3}} = 100.0 \space g \\
> c \space (\text{of solution}) &= 4.18 \space \mathrm{J\,g^{-1}\,K^{-1}} \\
> \Delta T \space (\text{of solution}) &= 29.2 \mathrm{°C} - 22.5 \mathrm{°C} = 6.7 \mathrm{°C} = 6.7 \mathrm{\,K} \\ \\
> \end{align}
> $$
> **Step 2:** Calculate the heat absorbed by the solution using $q=mc\Delta T$.
> $$
> \large
> \begin{align}
> q &= mc\Delta T \\
> q &= (100.0 \space \mathrm{g}) \times (4.18 \space \mathrm{J\,g^{-1}\,K^{-1}}) \times (6.7 \space \mathrm{K}) \\
> q &= 2800.6 \mathrm{\,J}
> \end{align}
> $$
> **Step 3:** Calculate the number of moles of water ($n_{\mathrm{H_2O}}$) produced.
> - First, find the moles of the acid and base to determine the moles of water formed.
> $$
> \large
> \begin{align}
> \text{Moles of HCl} &= (1.0 \space \mathrm{mol\,dm^{-3}}) \times (0.0500 \space \mathrm{dm^3}) = 0.0500 \space \mathrm{mol} \\
> \text{Moles of NaOH} &= (1.0 \space \mathrm{mol\,dm^{-3}}) \times (0.0500 \space \mathrm{dm^3}) = 0.0500 \space \mathrm{mol}
> \end{align}
> $$
> - From the 1:1 stoichiometry of the balanced equation, 0.0500 mol of HCl reacts with 0.0500 mol of NaOH to produce 0.0500 mol of H₂O.
> $$
> \large
> n_{\mathrm{H_2O}} = 0.0500 \space \mathrm{mol}
> $$
> 
> **Step 4:** Calculate the molar enthalpy of neutralisation using $\Delta H_{\text{neut}}^{\ominus} = \large\frac{-q}{n_{\mathrm{H_2O}}}$
> - The heat *absorbed by the solution* ($q$) is equal in magnitude but opposite in sign to the heat *released by the reaction*.
> $$
> \large
> \begin{align}
> \Delta H_{\text{neut}}^{\ominus} &= \frac{-q}{n_{\mathrm{H_2O}}} \\
> \Delta H_{\text{neut}}^{\ominus} &= \frac{-2800.6 \space \mathrm{J}}{0.0500 \space \mathrm{mol}} \\
> \Delta H_{\text{neut}}^{\ominus} &= -56012 \space \mathrm{J\,mol^{-1}}
> \end{align}
> $$
> 
> **Step 5:** Convert the answer to $\mathrm{kJ\,mol^{-1}}$ and apply significant figures.
> - The data with the fewest significant figures are the concentrations (1.0 $\mathrm{mol\,dm^{-3}}$) and the temperature change ($\Delta T = 6.7 \mathrm{°C}$), which both have two significant figures.
> $$
> \large \frac{-56012}{1000} = -56.012 \mathrm{\,kJ\,mol^{-1}}
> $$
> Rounding to two significant figures gives:
> $$
> \large
> \Delta H_{\text{neut}}^{\ominus} = -56 \mathrm{\,kJ\,mol^{-1}}
> $$
