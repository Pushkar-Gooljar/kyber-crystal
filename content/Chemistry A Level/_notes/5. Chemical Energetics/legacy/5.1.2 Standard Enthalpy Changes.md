
# Standard Conditions
- The precise amount of heat given out or taken in by a reaction varies with conditions. (e.g. Temperature, Pressures, Concentration, etc.)
- To fairly compare the changes in enthalpy between reactions, all reactions should be carried out under **standard conditions** (under the same conditions).
- Theses standard conditions are:
	- A **pressure** of 101 kPa
	- A **temperature** of 298 K (25°C)
	- Each substance involved in the reaction is in its **normal physical state** (solid, liquid, or gas) at 101 kPa and 298 K
- Standard conditions are shown by the symbol '⦵' placed as a superscript (e.g. $E^⦵$, $H^⦵$, etc.)

---

# Enthalpy Definitions

## 1. Standard Enthalpy Change of Reaction, $\Delta H^{⦵}_{r}$
>[!definition]
>- Enthalpy change when the amount of reactants shown in the stoichiometric equation react to form products 
>- ...under standard conditions.

> [!example] 
> $$
> \ce{ 2H_{2}_{(g)} + O_{2}_{(g)} -> 2H_{2}O_{(l)} }\space, \Delta H^{⦵}_{r} =-572 \space \mathrm{kJ\,mol}^{-1}
> $$
> **What does this mean?**
> When 2 moles of hydrogen gas reacts with 1 mol of oxygen gas to produce 2 moles of liquid water, 572 kJ of energy is released.

**Multiplying the stoichiometric equation by a constant $A$ also multiplies the $\Delta H^{⦵}_{r}$ by that constant**
>[!example]
> Let's say we have the equation from above:
> $$
> \ce{ 2H_{2}_{(g)} + O_{2}_{(g)} -> 2H_{2}O_{(l)} }\space, \Delta H^{⦵}_{r} =-572 \space \mathrm{kJ\,mol}^{-1}
> $$
> If we multiply this equation by $\frac{1}{2}$ (divide by 2), we get the equation below:
> $$
> \ce{ H_{2}_{(g)} + \frac{1}{2}O_{2}_{(g)} -> H_{2}O_{(l)} }\space, \Delta H^{⦵}_{r} =-286 \space \mathrm{kJ\,mol}^{-1}
> $$
> Notice how the $\Delta H^{⦵}_{r}$ also got multiplied by $\frac{1}{2}$.

## 2. Standard Enthalpy Change of Formation, $\Delta{{H}_{f}}^⦵$
> [!definition]
> - Enthalpy change when one mole of a compound is formed 
> - ...from its constituent elements 
> - ...with all substances in their standard states under standard conditions.
> 

- **Standard enthalpy change of formation can be both exothermic and endothermic**
>[!example]
> The formation of carbon dioxide is **exothermic**:
> $$
> \ce{ C_{(\text{graphite})} + O_{2}_{(g)} -> CO_{2}_{(g)}}\space ,\Delta{{H}_{f}}^⦵ = -393.5\space \mathrm{kJ\,mol}^{-1}
> $$
> While the formation of acetylene is **endothermic**:
> $$
> \ce{ 2C_{\text{(graphite)}} + H_{2}_{(g)} -> C_{2}H_{2}_{(g)} }\space \Delta{{H}_{f}}^⦵ = +226.7 \space \mathrm{kJ\,mol}^{-1}
> $$

- **The standard enthalpy change of formation of an element (e.g. $\ce{Ni, Ga, F_{2}, O_{2}}$ , etc.) is zero**
</br>

## 3. Standard Enthalpy Change of Combustion, $\Delta H^{⦵}_{c}$
>[!definition]
>- Enthalpy change when one mole of a substance 
>- ...completely burns in excess oxygen
>- ...under standard conditions.

- The substances combusted are either elements or compounds
>[!example]
> The combustion of sulfur:
> 
> $$
> \ce{ S_{(s)} +  O_{2}_{(g)} -> SO_{2}_{(g)}}\space ,\Delta H^{⦵}_{c}=-296.8 \space\mathrm{kJ\,mol}^{-1}
> $$
> The combustion of methane:
> $$
> \ce{ CH_{4}_{(g)} + 2O_{2}_{(g)} -> CO_{2}_{(g)} + 2H_{2}O_{(l)} }\space \Delta H^{⦵}_{c}=-890.3 \space \mathrm{kJ\,mol}^{-1}
> $$
> 
- Always **exothermic** as heat is always evolved in combustion 

## 4. Standard Enthalpy Change of Neutralisation, $\Delta H^{⦵}_{\text{neut}}$
>[!definition]
>- Enthalpy change when one mole of water is formed 
>- ...by the reaction of an **aqueous** acid and alkali 
>- ...under standard conditions.


- The value is slightly less for weak acids, because some energy is needed to dissociate the acids.
>[!example]
> The neutralisation reaction of hydrochloric acid with sodium hydroxide: (strong acid + strong base):
> $$
> \ce{ NaOH_{(aq)} + HCl_{(aq)} -> NaCl_{(aq)} + H_{2}O_{(l)} }\space, \Delta H^{⦵}_{\text{neut}}=-57.3\space \mathrm{kJ\,mol}^{-1}
> $$
> The neutralisation reaction of ethanoic acid with sodium hydroxide: (weak acid + strong base):
> $$
> \ce{ CH_{3}COOH_{(aq)} + NaOH_{(aq)} -> CH_{3}COONa_{(aq)} + H_{2}O_{(l)}  }\space, \Delta H^{⦵}_{\text{neut}}=-50.6\space \mathrm{kJ\,mol}^{-1}
> $$

- Always **exothermic** as more energy is released by the formation of $\ce{ O-H }$ bonds than energy absorbed to break bonds in acid and alkali.
